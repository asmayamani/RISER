Appendix A. Running Example — AI-Powered Student Assessment Stakeholder Analysis

This supplementary material provides the complete output of one execution of the stakeholder analysis pipeline applied to the AI-powered student assessment case study described in the main paper. The pipeline generated 159 stakeholders, evaluated them using Mitchell’s salience model, clustered them into 32 groups, and labelled them across an 11-category stakeholder groups.

A.1 System Description

An AI-powered student assessment system that uses machine learning to evaluate student performance, predict learning outcomes, and provide personalized recommendations for educational interventions. The system analyzes student data including test scores, attendance records, and behavioral patterns to identify at-risk students. The system described here sits at an uncomfortable intersection: it processes intimate data about children, test scores, attendance records, behavioural patterns, and uses it to make predictions that shape the kind of educational support a student receives.

What follows is a complete record of one run of the pipeline on this project.

A.2 Stage 01 — Generation

A.2.1 What the ensemble produced

Running the multi-model ensemble (each model called up to three times at varying temperatures) yielded 159 accepted stakeholders after cosine-similarity deduplication at a threshold of 0.8.

Mention counts are the most important attribute but useful signal. Students with disabilities came up 40 times across all model calls, more than any other role. Teachers came second at 35. That ordering is defensible: a system that flags students as at-risk ought to be scrutinised most carefully from the perspective of the students least equipped to contest its decisions. The drop-off is steep after the first ten or so roles; most stakeholders appear fewer than eight times, which is where the models begin generating roles that are genuine but less obvious, district data governance board members, FERPA compliance officers, educational psychologists as development team participants.

A.3 Stage 02 — Salience Evaluation

A.3.1 The scores and what they reveal

DeepSeek, considered after benchmarking, evaluated each stakeholder twice: first in isolation, scoring Legitimacy, Power, and Urgency on a 0–5 scale, then again calibrated against the full list. 131 of the 159 came back Valid; the 28 flagged redundant were dismissed on grounds of overlap.

The mean Legitimacy score across the 40 cluster representatives is 4.60, close to the ceiling. That's expected here: almost every stakeholder in an educational AI context has a plausible claim of rights or interests. The mean Power was at 2.77 as the pipeline uncovered different student groups that have full legitimacy, maximum urgency, minimal power to contest the decisions that affect them.

A.4 Stage 03 — Clustering

A.4.1 32 clusters, six with multiple representatives

The clustering took place to highlight different stakeholder groups to structure stakeholder engagement with representatives. In addition to the most frequent mentioned per cluster, any stakeholder mentioned more than 10 times (one quarter of the 40-mention maximum) was automatically promoted to representative alongside the highest-frequency item in their cluster. Six clusters ended up with more than one representative.

A.6 Stage 04 — Stakeholder Groups

A.6.1 Label distribution across representatives

Vulnerable Stakeholders and Data Subjects together account for 33 of the 81 labels assigned, just over 40% of the total. That concentration is coherent. The same people whose data the system processes are also most exposed to its errors. The pipeline assigns multi-label by design, and those co-assignments are themselves an ethics signal, a single stakeholder simultaneously serving as data source and subject of potential harm.

Civil Society received no labels. Advocacy and NGO-adjacent roles did appear in the list of representatives, yet it appeared within Cluster 11, as it contains civil rights organisation representatives, privacy advocates, ethicists, equity specialists.

Research & Scientific Community received a single label. That probably reflects the project framing: an operational tool rather than a research platform, which didn't draw many academic or benchmarking roles from the models.
